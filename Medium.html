Drawings, Prints, and Watercolors

Medium

  • When multiple media have been used, list the media in order of descending prevalence. For instance, “Transparent and opaque watercolor, graphite, and collage elements on cardboard.”
  • Use the term “opaque watercolor” in place of “gouache” and distinct from “transparent watercolor.”
  • Use the term “gummed varnishes” in place of the traditional term “gum arabic glaze.”
  • Media data should describe the type of media and not the method of application, e.g. “(description) ink,” not “pen and ink” nor “brushed ink.”
  • Felt tip pen is more accurately described as “porous point pen” due to its recognizable and distinctive appearance. The same guideline applies to “ballpoint pen.”
  • Append any additional qualifying information at the end of the description of media: e.g. “with scraping out”; “modified with gum varnish”; “with opaque white highlights”; “with touches of pastel.”
  • Description of medium must differentiate between media used as underdrawing and media deliberately used as part of the design. Proper designation is “media over graphite,” “media over charcoal,” or “media over black chalk.” This may be difficult to determine without a microscope. If in doubt, do not make entry until confirmed by subsequent investigation.

Paper Type and Support

For prints, drawings, and other works on paper, record Paper Type in the Support field and Watermark in the Watermark field.

Type “Paper Type:” and then record the color, thickness, texture, and weave type, in that specific order. For reference terminology, use the Print Council of America (PCA) paper sample book in the Registrar’s Office. Designate “wove” or “laid” despite the PCA’s omission of said characteristic.

Designate obvious changes and/or additions to the original support with the phrase “mounted to [new support].” Make every effort to distinguish between objects executed on a paper board support, objects later mounted to a paper board support, and objects applied to a paper board support through treatment. In the case of an obviously original paper board support, describe the board in the space normally devoted to paper, separated by the media by the word “on.” Use the term “Layered Paperboard” for bristol board, illustration board, and binders board.

It is often difficult to determine which parts of the support are considered original. Such a determination may require additional research and discussion. If ambiguity exists, record an initial description of the support and record the ambiguity in Discussion.

Paintings

For paintings, record medium and support in the Medium field. Record any additional technical notes or detailed medium descriptions in the Medium Notes field. The following terms represent the most common types of nineteenth and early twentieth century painting materials that may be encountered in ACM collections. Note that these terms are subject to change and should not set an editorial standard.

Medium

  • Oil – A drying oil used as a paint medium
  • Tempera – A water-based medium, which, upon drying, produces an opaque surface with a soft sheen. Medium Notes may further identify medium as egg tempera, made primarily from egg yolk, or glue tempera, also referred to as distemper.
  • Acrylic – A water- and polymer-based medium that is essentially plastic in nature.
  • Mixed media – Occasionally used to identify the use of a variety of media in a painting. However, every attempt should be made to identify specific media, with the principal medium given first. For example, “oil and tempera on canvas” indicates that the painting is principally oil with some tempera applied.

Support

  • Canvas – Generally refers to any type of fabric support. Medium Notes may further identify the canvas as linen, hemp, or cotton, and may also refer to the canvas texture (fine to coarse) and weave (plain, twill, or herringbone).
  • Panel – Generally refers to a wood panel, but may also be made of other materials, such as aluminum or copper. Medium Notes may further identify the type or wood or any other details about the panel’s material.
  • Paperboard or Layered paperboard – In the context of painting support, “paperboard” generally refers to cardboard.
  • Composition board – Medium Notes may further identify a trade name, such as masonite or artists’ fabric covered prepared boards.

Due to conservation treatments, a painting may have more than one support. For instance, a work that is oil on canvas may be mounted to an aluminum panel. The original support should be listed first, with the additional support listed second, i.e., “oil on canvas mounted to aluminum.” It is unnecessary to record the secondary support of a painting on canvas that has been lined with canvas, i.e., the medium is still “oil on canvas,” not “oil on canvas mounted to canvas.”
